# Behavioral Dynamics Reference — Generic B2B SaaS

**Purpose:** Framework for reading the human dynamics of a sales call — trust signals,
emotional mismatches, champion health, and what the prospect signaled but didn't say.
Used to populate the "The Unsaid" section and Participant Dynamics in Intelligence Briefs.

---

## Overview

Behavioral Dynamics analysis is a second layer of insight that sits alongside
MEDDPICC and SPICED. Where those frameworks assess *what was said*, Behavioral
Dynamics assesses *how it was said* — the subtext, the hesitation, the moments
of unexpected engagement or withdrawal.

This analysis is especially valuable on calls where:
- There's a disconnect between what the prospect says and how they behave
- A champion goes quiet or disengages mid-call
- Multiple stakeholders show different levels of enthusiasm
- The seller misreads a buying signal as confirmation when it's actually deflection

---

## Three Dimensions

### 1. Trust Trajectory

**What it measures:** Whether trust between seller and prospect is building,
holding steady, or eroding over the course of the call.

**Trust-building signals:**
- Prospect volunteers information beyond what was asked
- Prospect asks candid questions ("How do you compare to Vercel — honestly?")
- Prospect shares internal context (org dynamics, budget constraints, politics)
- Prospect engages with the seller as a thought partner, not just a vendor
- Laughter, informal asides, or "off the record" moments

**Trust erosion signals:**
- Prospect becomes shorter in answers as the call progresses
- Hedging language increases: "maybe," "we'll see," "it depends"
- Prospect stops asking questions
- Prospect defers or deflects: "Let me think about it and get back to you"
- Polite disengagement: answers questions technically correct but doesn't elaborate
- Prospect asks to be sent materials ("just send me the docs") — often an exit signal

**Trust holding steady:**
- Consistent engagement throughout
- Answers are factual and complete but not expansive
- Professional tone maintained, no warmth but no coldness

**Scoring:** Improving / Stable / Declining / Mixed

---

### 2. Emotional Mismatch

**What it measures:** Moments where what the prospect says doesn't match the emotional
register they're operating in — or where the seller's tone is misaligned with the
prospect's state.

**Prospect-side mismatches:**
- Verbal enthusiasm + short answers: "That sounds interesting" + gives minimal detail
- Agreeing while showing disengagement: "Sure, yes, makes sense" in rapid succession without elaboration
- Claiming urgency while being vague on next steps: "We need to move fast" + "I'll get back to you"
- Expressing concern once, then acting as if it was resolved without resolution
- Asking a lot of questions while explicitly saying "we're not ready to evaluate yet"

**Seller-side mismatches:**
- Pitching while the prospect is still describing the problem
- Celebrating agreement on a point that the prospect was actually lukewarm on
- Going deep into features when the prospect is asking about process/support
- Treating a polite "that's interesting" as buying intent
- Pushing for next steps on a timeline the prospect's body language (or verbal cues) has rejected

**Why this matters:** Emotional mismatches often predict deal risk before it shows up in
qualification data. A prospect who is politely going through the motions is a different
situation from one who is genuinely engaged — even if the MEDDPICC scores look similar.

---

### 3. Champion Health

**What it measures:** The strength, motivation, and internal positioning of the person
acting as your champion inside the prospect organization.

**Strong champion signals:**
- Shares information you'd only get from an insider: budget visibility, internal dynamics, stakeholder hesitations
- Takes internal initiative: "I've already talked to our CTO about this"
- Describes the problem in language that shows they've been living with it
- Has a personal stake in the outcome: "This is my initiative — I want to own the modernization project"
- Protects your access: "Let me make sure you get time with the right people"
- Articulates why they want this to move forward: "I'm tired of the manual process — I want this fixed"

**Weak or deteriorating champion signals:**
- Becomes less specific over time — early calls had detail, recent calls are vague
- Deflects requests to involve other stakeholders: "I'll handle that internally"
- Stops advocating and starts gatekeeping: "I'll let you know what the team thinks"
- Uses "we" language that implies committee thinking when earlier it was "I" and "my initiative"
- Misses committed next steps without explanation
- Champion's title or role has shifted — no longer in position to drive the decision
- Apologetic tone: "I'm sorry I haven't had a chance to follow up..."

**Champion absent:**
- Single contact who provides information but shows no advocacy behavior
- Contact explicitly says "I'm just gathering information for the team"
- No evidence that the contact is selling internally on your behalf

## The Unsaid — Writing This Section

The Unsaid is a 2-3 sentence behavioral read at the end of each Intelligence Brief.
It answers: *"What did the prospect signal but not say directly?"*

**What it is:**
- A sharp, specific behavioral observation
- Grounded in observable signals from the call (language, patterns, timing)
- Actionable: the observation should change how the seller approaches the next interaction

**What it is not:**
- A summary of what the prospect said
- Speculation without evidence
- A list of things the AE/SE could have done better (that goes in Coaching Spotlight)

**Good examples:**

> "The prospect's enthusiasm for the feature demo didn't translate into any forward motion —
> no questions about pricing, implementation, or next steps. This engagement pattern
> suggests curiosity, not urgency. The deal is exploratory until proven otherwise."

> "When pricing was mentioned, the prospect's answers became shorter and more hedged.
> The phrase 'we'd need to look at that separately' appeared twice — a signal that budget
> authority likely sits above this contact and hasn't been engaged."

> "The champion gave notably specific answers about the pain but deflected every question
> about internal stakeholders. This suggests either limited visibility into the decision
> process or a political situation they're managing. Probing the org dynamics on the
> next call is more valuable than deepening the technical discussion."

**Bad examples (too vague or too judgmental):**

> "The prospect seems interested but not ready to move forward." ← No behavioral evidence
> "The AE should have asked better questions." ← That's coaching, not a behavioral read
> "It's unclear whether this deal will close." ← Every deal is unclear until it closes

## Participant Dynamics Section (Extended Calls)

For calls with significant behavioral signals — tension, misalignment, or clear dynamics
between multiple participants — include a Participant Dynamics section after The Unsaid.

**Format:**

```
## Participant Dynamics

| Participant | Role | Engagement Arc | Key Moment |
|-------------|------|---------------|------------|
| [Name] | [Title] | [Improving/Stable/Declining] | [Most revealing moment] |
| [Name] | [Title] | [Improving/Stable/Declining] | [Most revealing moment] |

**Tension observed:** [1-2 sentences on any visible friction between participants,
or between participants and sellers]

**Implication for next call:** [What to do differently given these dynamics]
```

---

## When to Apply Behavioral Analysis

Behavioral analysis adds the most value on calls with:
- Multiple stakeholders showing different levels of engagement
- Any sign of internal tension (disagreement, deference, avoidance)
- A champion who seems less engaged than in prior calls
- A prospect who gave verbal agreement but avoided committing to next steps
- Significant emotional moments (frustration, enthusiasm, defensiveness)

On pure technical scoping calls with no notable dynamics, The Unsaid can be brief
("no significant behavioral signals — call was technical and straightforward") or omitted.
